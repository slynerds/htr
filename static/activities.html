Resilient Productive Landscapes

The RPL project will break the cycle of natural resources degradation and increasing vulnerability among rural families through an integrated approach in four watersheds in the Nippes department. RPL will work at both the farm and community level to simultaneously raise production and productivity using more resilient agriculture practices adapted to agro-ecological zones, which will directly contribute to rebuilding the natural resources base. Support for local production will include inputs like seeds, plants and training as well as support to get products to market, including rural infrastructure.

RPL will also strengthen the institutional enabling environment and build capacity for landscape level governance and management at both national and local levels. Interventions were proposed and vetted through a participatory process that included all local stakeholders and elected officials.

Resilient… the ability to withstand the shocks that come with a changing climate
Productive… the capacity of the land to produce enough for families to survive and thrive
Landscapes… the perspective of a watershed-level, ridge-to-reef strategy as a framework for all interventions

Market-Linked Reforestation in Nippes

The approach is simple: in order to have environmental successes, activities must have a guaranteed positive economic impact. With support from J/P HRO, Acceso conducted a value chain assessment in order to understand what food staples are being produced in the western Nippes department as well as what agroforestry products have export potential.

Recognizing that economic pressures on rural families make it difficult for them to allow trees to mature into the money-making resources they can be, Acceso helps farmers invest in long-term production assets including micro-orchards while integrating production of tree crops that have a guaranteed market, like moringa leaves destined to become nutrient-rich powder. PILOT: Payment for Ecosystem Services

In the community of Sault du Baril, deforestation and constant cultivation have left land vulnerable to severe erosion, especially during heavy rains and tropical storms. The MODESBA farmers’ organization is seeing the impact on the local water system, especially the cascade of waterfall

To restore some of the most denuded land in the community, MODESBA is piloting a program where local landowners provide fencing and in return they are receiving tree seedlings and training, as well as payments that will enable them to allow the trees to mature to make a lasting ecological impact including soil enrichment and erosion control.

PILOT: Enhanced Agroforestry in Nippes

As part of the preparation for the Resilient Productive Landscapes (RPL) project, farmers in the four watersheds participated in workshops to determine the most important agricultural and infrastructure interventions for their region. In community after community, farmers explained that for decades, key limes were one of the most important products in the NIppes department. The value chain included the local market, where people rely on the strong citrus juice for preparation of meats and fish, to the export market, where Haitian key lime oil is considered a very valuable cooking ingredient.

In May 2018, a pilot program was launched to field test a new strategy for protecting the lime trees. In order to create what is called a phytorifulsive barrier, guava trees are planted around the lime trees, creating a line that pests won’t cross. So far, 1,120 key lime trees have been planted with 120 guava trees to protect them. And because the pilot has already begun to prove that this technical approach can be successful, more key lime trees will be added until a total of 5,000 lime trees are healthy and growing in the region.

PILOT: Canaan Urban Greening

It was in the aftermath of the January 2010 earthquake when people began building in the vast area known as Canaan, and they created their city without any support from the Haitian government or NGOs. Rapid urbanization in an environmentally vulnerable area comes with many challenges, but communities carved out spaces for playing fields and public parks, a delineated a web of roads.

In partnership with local community leadership known as Neighborhood Committees, Acceso social enterprise, and American Red Cross, J/P HRO piloted three innovative strategies:

  1. Urban agroforestry: Support to individuals in Canaan to start small tree nurseries and to plant in no-build zones as well as other vulnerable areas. Combining livelihood enhancement with environmental protection
  2. Green and Gray Infrastructure: In one of the largest ravines in Canaan, a combination of traditional ravine correction tactics combined with green infrastructure including trees and grasses have had a visible impact in preventing flooding
  3. Mini-Forests: In this method, a very dense patch of trees, shrubs and grasses/cacti are planted together to mimic a forest. The strategy creates an ecological system where one plant supports another, and where ground cover helps to retain moisture, while an outer ring of tough grasses and cacti prevent goats and other animals from eating the trees

The strategies in Canaan have been pulled together to form the basis of an urban greening toolkit. Read more on our blog
